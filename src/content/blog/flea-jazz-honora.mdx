## Acid for the children

To understand this shift, you have to go back to his roots, beautifully 
detailed in his autobiography *Acid for the Children*. Long before the rock 
fame, Flea’s identity was shaped by his family history. Honora, his British 
grandmother who made the massive leap to emigrate to Australia, represents 
the very beginning of his lineage. Her presence on the album cover is a direct 
portal into the childhood memories that forged his soul.

But the catalyst for his musical awakening came later. After his parents 
separated, Flea grew up in a chaotic music-filled home driven by his stepfather, 
Walter Urban Jr. This passionate jazz musician didn't just give the young boy 
a taste for the genre, he taught him to listen to the freedom within it. 
That early exposure to the genius of Louis Armstrong and Miles Davis became 
Flea's true foundation — a sanctuary he is finally returning to today, 
trumpet in hand.

## The vulnerability of a rock star

This deep-rooted respect for the genre explains why Flea approached this 
project not as a rock star playing dress-up, but with absolute humility. 
During his promotional run, notably in a deeply moving
[interview with Rick Beato](https://youtu.be/M6fJ5Nx_X5k?si=kju8LW35hSTtJW9S), 
we saw a side of the bassist the public rarely witnesses. 
Overwhelmed with gratitude, he broke down in tears as the seasoned jazz 
masters sharing the studio welcomed him not as an outsider, 
but as a true musical equal.

It’s a striking reality check: global rock stardom doesn't cure the deep-seated 
need for artistic validation. It requires immense humility to strip away the 
ego, sit in front of your peers, and just hope your notes are honest enough.

## Inside the tracklist: from heavyweights to "Maggot Brain"

This humble approach is exactly what drew other alternative icons to the 
project. *Honora* features unexpected collaborations with heavyweights like 
Thom Yorke and Nick Cave. Their presence never feels like a commercial stunt; 
they are simply there to help Flea shape the album's sound.

Another major highlight of the tracklist is his bold reinterpretation of 
Funkadelic’s "Maggot Brain". Originally an iconic, weeping guitar solo by 
Eddie Hazel, Flea completely transforms it. He strips away the distortion and 
reinvents the piece through his trumpet, honoring his funk-rock past while 
firmly pushing into this new territory.

## "The Plea": a message of peace

Beyond the guest stars and the bold covers, the true heart of *Honora* lies in 
its authenticity. This is clear right from the opening track, "The Plea", 
where the album's great technical playing suddenly gives way to something much 
more raw. The real shock comes past the 3:33 mark, when Flea simply lays down 
his trumpet to sing a message about love and connection.

Guided by the spirits of his past, there are no gimmicks here — just a man, 
his history, and a profound feeling of peace.
